# Domain Coverage Checklist: Israeli Fertility Treatment

Anchor list for reviewing this skill's completeness. Scope: navigating fertility
treatment in Israel (coverage, rights, law, costs, support), not clinical care.

## Must cover (core)

1. IVF in the health basket, basic eligibility. IVF is a basket service every kupat cholim must provide under the National Health Insurance Law 1994. Source: health.gov.il fertility pages; Clalit IVF rights page.
2. The 2-live-births rule. The basket funds IVF up to two children from the woman's current relationship or family unit. A woman with children from a previous partner remains eligible for two with her current partner or as a single mother. Source: Clalit, Maccabi eligibility 2565, Meuhedet.
3. Age-45 ceiling for funded own-egg IVF (a commitment given at 44 must be realized before the official 45th birthday). Source: Maccabi eligibility 2565.
4. No-cycle-cap nuance. The limit is the outcome (two live births) and age, not a count of attempts. State this explicitly because it is counterintuitive. Source: Maccabi 2565; israelivf.co.il.
5. IUI vs ICSI vs IVF distinguished, all basket-covered when indicated. Source: kupot eligibility pages.
6. PGT/PGD coverage. Funded with committee approval for defined indications; the 2026 basket added PGT as part of IVF for carriers of high-cancer-risk mutations (BRCA1/BRCA2, Lynch). Source: 2026 basket protocols; gov.il PGD page.
7. SHABAN supplementary fertility benefits (extra cycles beyond two children, choice of doctor, shorter waits, premium meds), with waiting periods. Source: each kupah's SHABAN pages.
8. Medical egg freezing / fertility preservation, basket-funded for oncology patients (before chemo or radiation), women up to 39 at elevated early-menopause risk, gender-affirming care, and specific genetic indications (fragile-X premutation carriers get the higher cap). Standard cap 4 cycles or 20 eggs; higher cap 6 cycles or 40 eggs for those genetic indications. Source: Clalit service bag; Maccabi eligibility 1834.
9. Elective / social egg freezing, NOT basket-funded (self-pay), per the 2011 Ministry of Health directive. Several kupot now subsidize it via SHABAN (Clalit, Maccabi, Meuhedet); Leumit does not. State price bands and tell users to confirm with the kupah. Source: MoH 2011 directive; ynet coverage of SHABAN subsidies.
10. Sperm donation, eligibility (single women, couples, each partner in a female same-sex couple) and mandatory anonymity by law. A 2025 public committee (Mor-Yosef) is examining an identified-donation track. Source: Kol Zchut sperm donation; health.gov.il sperm-bank procedure.
11. Egg donation under the Egg Donation Law 2010, recipients basket-funded within the standard age window, donors compensated, committee approval. Source: health.gov.il egg donation; Knesset law.
12. Surrogacy under the Embryo Carrying Agreements Law 1996. Gestational only, statutory approvals committee, surrogate must be an Israeli resident aged 22-39 who has given birth and is not related to the intended parents. Source: gov.il embryo-carrying service.
13. The 2022 surrogacy reform (effective 11 January 2022) opened surrogacy to single men, same-sex couples, and transgender people following High Court rulings. Date it precisely. Source: gov.il; press coverage.
14. Single mother by choice, IVF and sperm-donation eligibility without a partner. Source: kupot eligibility; Kol Zchut.
15. Lesbian co-mother parenthood, each partner may receive donor sperm and the non-biological mother's parenthood is established via a judicial parenthood order; a March 2024 Supreme Court ruling requires registering both as mothers on the birth certificate. Source: Kol Zchut; Supreme Court ruling coverage.
16. Fertility-treatment work leave under the Women's Employment Regulations (Absence for Fertility and IVF Treatments) 1990, up to 16 days per series (5-day week) or 20 days (6-day week), max 4 series per year, against sick days, with advance notice and medical confirmation. Source: Kol Zchut; Women's Employment Law.
17. Anti-dismissal protection under the Women's Employment Law, no dismissal during fertility-treatment absence and for 150 days after, without a Ministry of Labor permit. Source: Kol Zchut; gov.il worker-rights page.
18. Cost picture, what is covered vs out-of-pocket (third child without SHABAN, private clinics, elective freezing, storage, surrogacy total), with figures clearly labelled as estimates. Source: kupot; Kol Zchut.
19. Emotional support, fertility-unit social workers, support organizations, kupah groups. Source: Kol Zchut; support NGOs.

## Should cover (advanced)

1. Frozen embryo transfer (FET) coverage and the difference from a fresh cycle.
2. SHABAN waiting periods and per-child caps (the volatile pieces, re-verify against live circulars).
3. Fertility preservation for cancer patients named explicitly as the largest medical-indication group.
4. Importing donor sperm or eggs from abroad (open-identity workaround).
5. Embryo disposition disputes on divorce or separation (Nahmani precedent and later case law).
6. Religious or halachic dimension, supervision of gamete handling and donor-religion selection.
7. Bituach Leumi interface, treatment funding is via the kupot and basket while maternity and related benefits sit with the National Insurance Institute.
8. Choice of fertility unit and wait-time differences between hospitals.
9. The pending identified-sperm-donation reform (Mor-Yosef committee) as a watch item.

## Out of scope (explicit)

- Personalized medical advice, diagnosis, medication or protocol choice (defer to the treating physician and IVF unit).
- Real-time price quotes for specific private clinics (cite bands only, confirm with the clinic or kupah).
- Step-by-step legal drafting of surrogacy or parentage agreements (refer to a family-law attorney and the approvals committee).
- Adoption, a distinct legal track under different law.
- Abortion or pregnancy-termination committees, a separate domain.
- Mental-health treatment delivery (point to support resources only; defer therapy to a mental-health navigator skill and hotlines).
